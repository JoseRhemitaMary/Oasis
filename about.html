The Taj Mahal (/ˌtɑːdʒ məˈhɑːl, ˌtɑːʒ -/ TAHJ mə-HAHL, TAHZH -⁠, Hindi: [taːdʒ ˈmɛɦ(ɛ)l]; lit. 'Crown of the Palace') is an ivory-white marble mausoleum on the right bank of the river Yamuna in Agra, Uttar Pradesh, India. It was commissioned in 1631 by the fifth Mughal emperor, Shah Jahan (r. 1628–1658) to house the tomb of his beloved wife, Mumtaz Mahal; it also houses the tomb of Shah Jahan himself. The tomb is the centrepiece of a 17-hectare (42-acre) complex, which includes a mosque and a guest house, and is set in formal gardens bounded on three sides by a crenellated wall.The Taj Mahal is located on the right bank of the Yamuna River in a vast Mughal garden that encompasses nearly 17 hectares, in the Agra District in Uttar Pradesh. It was built by Mughal Emperor Shah Jahan in memory of his wife Mumtaz Mahal with construction starting in 1632 AD and completed in 1648 AD, with the mosque, the guest house and the main gateway on the south, the outer courtyard and its cloisters were added subsequently and completed in 1653 AD. The existence of several historical and Quaranic inscriptions in Arabic script have facilitated setting the chronology of Taj Mahal. For its construction, masons, stone-cutters, inlayers, carvers, painters, calligraphers, dome builders and other artisans were requisitioned from the whole of the empire and also from the Central Asia and Iran. Ustad-Ahmad Lahori was the main architect of the Taj Mahal. The Taj Mahal is considered to be the greatest architectural achievement in the whole range of Indo-Islamic architecture. Its recognised architectonic beauty has a rhythmic combination of solids and voids, concave and convex and light shadow; such as arches and domes further increases the aesthetic aspect. The colour combination of lush green scape reddish pathway and blue sky over it show cases the monument in ever changing tints and moods. The relief work in marble and inlay with precious and semi precious stones make it a monument apart.

The Red Fort Complex was built as the palace fort of Shahjahanabad – the new capital of the fifth Mughal Emperor of India, Shah Jahan. Named for its massive enclosing walls of red sandstone, it is adjacent to an older fort, the Salimgarh, built by Islam Shah Suri in 1546, with which it forms the Red Fort Complex. The private apartments consist of a row of pavilions connected by a continuous water channel, known as the Nahr-i-Behisht (Stream of Paradise). The Red Fort is considered to represent the zenith of Mughal creativity which, under the Shah Jahan, was brought to a new level of refinement. The planning of the palace is based on Islamic prototypes, but each pavilion reveals architectural elements typical of Mughal building, reflecting a fusion of Persian, Timurid and Hindu traditions The Red Fort’s innovative planning and architectural style, including the garden design, strongly influenced later buildings and gardens in Rajasthan, Delhi, Agra and further afield.The Red Fort, or Lal Qila, is an iconic historic monument located in Delhi, India. Constructed in 1639 by the Mughal Emperor Shah Jahan, it served as the primary residence of Mughal rulers for nearly 200 years. Built from striking red sandstone, the fort showcases a blend of Persian, Timurid, and Indian architectural styles. It houses notable structures like the Lahori Gate, Diwan-i-Aam, Diwan-i-Khas, Rang Mahal, and Moti Masjid, each reflecting the grandeur of Mughal architecture. The Red Fort is also a symbol of India's independence, as the Prime Minister hoists the national flag here every year on August 15. Recognized as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2007, the fort remains a testament to India's rich history and is a major tourist attraction, hosting sound and light shows that narrate its legacy.

Hawa Mahal, or the "Palace of Winds," is a renowned landmark in Jaipur, Rajasthan. Built in 1799 by Maharaja Sawai Pratap Singh, it was designed by Lal Chand Ustad in the shape of Lord Krishna's crown. This stunning five-story palace is constructed from red and pink sandstone, embodying the essence of Rajput architecture. The structure features 953 intricately carved jharokhas (small windows) that allow cool breezes to flow through, making it an architectural marvel designed for ventilation. Originally built for royal women to observe street festivals and daily life without being seen, Hawa Mahal offers a glimpse into the opulence and cultural traditions of its era. Located near the City Palace and Jantar Mantar, it remains one of Jaipur’s most visited tourist attractions, offering panoramic views of the city and a timeless connection to Rajasthan’s rich heritage Share Hawa Mahal, also known as the "Palace of Winds," is a striking architectural gem located in the heart of Jaipur, Rajasthan. Commissioned in 1799 by Maharaja Sawai Pratap Singh, it was designed to resemble the crown of Lord Krishna, reflecting the devotion and cultural richness of the Rajput dynasty. The palace is renowned for its unique facade, featuring 953 small windows, or jharokhas, adorned with intricate latticework. This design not only adds to its aesthetic appeal but also serves a practical purpose by allowing cool air to circulate, creating a natural air-conditioning effect in the scorching desert climate.

**Humayun’s Tomb: A Masterpiece of Mughal Architecture** Humayun’s Tomb, located in Delhi, India, is a stunning example of Mughal architecture and a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Built in 1570 by Empress Bega Begum, the wife of Emperor Humayun, the tomb reflects a blend of Persian, Islamic, and Indian architectural styles. It was the first garden tomb in the Indian subcontinent and features symmetrical designs, intricate lattice work, and a striking double dome. Surrounded by lush Charbagh gardens divided into quadrants by water channels, the site exudes serenity and grandeur. Humayun’s Tomb also served as an inspiration for the design of the iconic Taj Mahal. Its historical and architectural significance makes it a must-visit destination for history enthusiasts and art lovers alike.Humayun’s Tomb, a jewel of Mughal architecture, stands majestically in the heart of Delhi, India. Commissioned by his first wife, Empress Bega Begum, in memory of the Mughal Emperor Humayun, the tomb was completed in 1572 under the guidance of Persian architect Mirak Mirza Ghiyas. This awe-inspiring structure marked the beginning of the Mughal architectural style in India, blending Persian elegance with Indian craftsmanship. The tomb is set within a sprawling Charbagh garden, a quintessential feature of Persian landscapes symbolizing paradise, with its quadrilateral layout of lush greenery and intersecting water channels. Built with red sandstone and white marble, the edifice boasts a towering double dome, ornate arches, and an intricate jaali (lattice) design, showcasing the artistic brilliance of its time. Humayun’s Tomb is not just an architectural masterpiece but also a historical treasure. It served as a prototype for later Mughal mausoleums, including the Taj Mahal. The tomb complex houses the graves of several Mughal royals, making it a family necropolis and a testimony to the rich history of the empire. Today, Humayun’s Tomb is a UNESCO World Heritage Site, drawing visitors from across the globe. Its timeless beauty, historical significance, and serene surroundings make it a symbol of love, legacy, and enduring craftsmanship. Whether you're a history enthusiast or a casual traveler, this magnificent monument promises an unforgettable experience.
